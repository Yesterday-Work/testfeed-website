Most market research projects fail not because the researcher lacks skill, but because there is no clear plan before the first question is written. A solid market research template fixes that. It forces you to define what you are trying to learn before you design how you will learn it, which saves time, money and a great deal of frustration when you sit down to analyse your results.

This post gives you a free market research template you can copy and use today, a filled example so you can see exactly how it works in practice, and guidance on adapting it to your specific situation.

## What this market research template is for

This market research template is designed for anyone who needs to plan and execute a research study from scratch. That includes startup founders validating a new product, marketing managers building a go-to-market plan, junior researchers preparing their first study, and students completing coursework or dissertations.

The template covers both **primary market research** (data you collect yourself, directly from your target audience) and **secondary market research** (data that already exists in reports, databases and public sources). It is format-agnostic: you can run it as a Word document, a Notion page, a Google Doc, or a simple spreadsheet. What matters is that you complete every section before you collect a single data point.

> "The biggest mistake in market research is starting with the method. Start with the question. Everything else follows from there."

### MARKET RESEARCH PLAN TEMPLATE

---

**PROJECT DETAILS**

| Field | Your entry |
|---|---|
| Project name | [Name of this research project] |
| Researcher / team | [Your name or team name] |
| Date started | [DD/MM/YYYY] |
| Target completion | [DD/MM/YYYY] |
| Budget | [Estimated total in your currency] |
| Stakeholder / decision-maker | [Who will act on these findings] |

---

**SECTION 1: RESEARCH OBJECTIVES**

*What are you trying to learn? Write 3 to 5 specific, measurable objectives.*

- Objective 1: [e.g. Understand the top three pain points experienced by our target customer when managing project timelines]
- Objective 2: [e.g. Identify which pricing tier is most acceptable to SME buyers in the UK]
- Objective 3: [e.g. Determine current awareness of our brand among first-time founders in Australia]
- Objective 4: [Optional]
- Objective 5: [Optional]

**Key research question (single sentence):** [What is the one question, if answered, that would make this project a success?]

---

**SECTION 2: TARGET AUDIENCE DEFINITION**

*Who are you researching? Be specific.*

- **Primary audience:** [Job title, age range, sector, geography, behaviour]
- **Secondary audience (if applicable):** [Any additional segment worth capturing]
- **Inclusion criteria:** [What makes someone eligible to participate in this study]
- **Exclusion criteria:** [Who should NOT be included, e.g. competitors, existing customers if you want prospect views]
- **Target sample size:** [Number of respondents or interview participants]
- **Recruitment method:** [How will you find participants? Panel provider, your own database, social media, etc.]

---

**SECTION 3: RESEARCH METHODOLOGY**

*Select and describe your approach.*

**Primary research methods (tick all that apply):**

- [ ] Online survey
- [ ] In-depth interview (IDI)
- [ ] Focus group
- [ ] Usability test / observation
- [ ] Diary study

**Secondary research methods (tick all that apply):**

- [ ] Industry and analyst reports (e.g. Statista, IBISWorld, Mintel)
- [ ] Government and census data
- [ ] Competitor website and content analysis
- [ ] Academic literature
- [ ] Social listening / forum mining (Reddit, Trustpilot, G2, etc.)
- [ ] Patent and product filings

**Chosen primary method:** [Describe in one paragraph why you have selected this method over alternatives and what its limitations are]

**Chosen secondary sources:** [List the specific reports, databases or sources you will draw on]

| Method | Strengths | Limitations | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Online survey | Fast, scalable, quantifiable | No follow-up probing | Measuring frequency, preference, awareness |
| In-depth interview | Rich context, uncovers nuance | Slow, expensive, small n | Understanding motivations and barriers |
| Focus group | Group dynamics surface unexpected views | Social desirability bias | Concept testing, creative feedback |
| Secondary research | Low cost, fast | Data may be dated or non-specific | Market sizing, competitive context |
| Synthetic audience | Instant, zero recruitment cost | Directional, not statistically representative | Early validation, instrument testing |

---

**SECTION 4: MARKET RESEARCH QUESTIONS**

*Write the questions you will ask participants. Organise by theme. Include question type in brackets.*

**Screener questions (to qualify respondents):**

1. [Screener Q1 — e.g. "Which of the following best describes your role?" Multiple choice with qualifying options] (Closed — multiple choice)
2. [Screener Q2 — e.g. "How often do you purchase software for your team?" with qualifying frequency] (Closed — frequency scale)

**Section A: Awareness and context**

3. [e.g. "Before today, had you heard of [brand/product/category]?"] (Closed — yes/no)
4. [e.g. "How did you first hear about it?"] (Closed — multiple choice)
5. [e.g. "How would you describe your current approach to [problem area]?"] (Open-ended)

**Section B: Pain points and needs**

6. [e.g. "What is your biggest challenge when [doing X]?"] (Open-ended)
7. [e.g. "On a scale of 1 to 10, how satisfied are you with your current solution for [problem]?"] (Closed — rating scale)
8. [e.g. "What would need to change about your current solution for you to rate it a 9 or 10?"] (Open-ended)

**Section C: Concept or product evaluation**

9. [e.g. "Having reviewed [concept/product description], how likely are you to [try / buy / recommend] it?"] (Closed — 5-point Likert)
10. [e.g. "What, if anything, appeals to you about this concept?"] (Open-ended)
11. [e.g. "What concerns, if any, do you have about it?"] (Open-ended)

**Section D: Pricing and value**

12. [e.g. "What price would you consider too expensive for this type of product?"] (Open-ended or numeric input)
13. [e.g. "What price would you consider too cheap — so cheap you would question the quality?"] (Open-ended or numeric input)
14. [e.g. "At what price would this represent good value for money?"] (Open-ended or numeric input)

**Section E: Demographics (always at the end)**

15. [Age range — closed, buckets]
16. [Company size or household income — closed, buckets]
17. [Industry or sector — closed, multiple choice]
18. [Anything else relevant to your audience definition]

---

**SECTION 5: DATA COLLECTION PLAN**

| Task | Owner | Start date | End date | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Finalise survey instrument | [Name] | [Date] | [Date] | Pilot test with 5 people first |
| Set up survey platform | [Name] | [Date] | [Date] | [Platform: Typeform, SurveyMonkey, etc.] |
| Recruit participants | [Name] | [Date] | [Date] | [Panel, outreach, social] |
| Field survey / run interviews | [Name] | [Date] | [Date] | Target n = [X] |
| Close fieldwork | [Name] | [Date] | [Date] | Allow buffer for slow responses |
| Export and clean data | [Name] | [Date] | [Date] | Remove incomplete responses |

---

**SECTION 6: ANALYSIS APPROACH**

- **Quantitative analysis:** [Describe how you will analyse closed-ended data. E.g. frequency distributions, cross-tabulations by segment, Net Promoter Score calculation, Van Westendorp price sensitivity analysis]
- **Qualitative analysis:** [Describe how you will handle open-ended responses. E.g. thematic coding, affinity mapping, sentiment tagging]
- **Tools:** [Excel, SPSS, Python, Dovetail, Aurelius, TestFeed, etc.]
- **Cross-tabs you plan to run:** [List the audience segments you will compare, e.g. by age, by company size, by geography]
- **Minimum reportable cell size:** [Define the smallest sub-group you will report on, typically n = 30 for quantitative, n = 5 for qualitative]

  - Executive summary (key findings and recommendations, max 1 page)

  - Findings by theme
  - Recommendations

- **Confidentiality level:** [Internal only / shareable externally / publishable]

## Filled example

Below is the template completed for a fictional product: **Stackly**, a task management app targeting freelance designers aged 25 to 40 in the UK.

---

**PROJECT DETAILS**

| Field | Entry |
|---|---|
| Project name | Stackly — Pre-launch audience validation |
| Researcher / team | Product team (2 researchers) |
| Date started | 01/06/2026 |
| Target completion | 25/06/2026 |
| Budget | £2,500 |
| Stakeholder | Head of Product, Jack Brennan |

---

**SECTION 1: RESEARCH OBJECTIVES**

- Objective 1: Identify the top three workflow pain points for freelance designers managing multiple clients simultaneously
- Objective 2: Gauge willingness to pay for a task management tool with client portal access at three price points (£9/mo, £15/mo, £25/mo)
- Objective 3: Determine whether "client portal" or "time tracking" is the more compelling feature for this audience
- Objective 4: Understand which existing tools (Notion, Trello, Asana, paper-based) are most commonly used and why people are dissatisfied

**Key research question:** What would cause a freelance designer to switch their current task management approach to a paid tool?

---

**SECTION 2: TARGET AUDIENCE DEFINITION**

- **Primary audience:** Freelance designers aged 25 to 40, based in the UK, who manage three or more active client projects at any given time
- **Inclusion criteria:** Self-employed or operating as a sole trader; design discipline (graphic, UX, motion, brand); generating at least £30,000 per year from freelance work
- **Exclusion criteria:** Agency employees; anyone who works on only one project at a time
- **Target sample size:** 150 survey respondents; 8 in-depth interviews
- **Recruitment method:** Design community Facebook groups, Dribbble network, panel provider for top-up

---

**SECTION 3: RESEARCH METHODOLOGY**

**Chosen primary methods:** Online survey (quantitative baseline) + in-depth interviews (qualitative depth). The survey will establish frequency and preference data across a large enough sample to be directional. The interviews will uncover the motivations and language behind the numbers.

**Chosen secondary sources:** Freelance industry report (IPSE 2025 Freelancer Survey); G2 and Capterra reviews for Notion, Trello and Asana (social listening); Companies House data on sole trader registrations in design.

---

**SECTION 4: MARKET RESEARCH QUESTIONS (selected)**

*Screener:*
1. "Are you currently working as a freelance designer?" (Yes/No — Yes required)
2. "How many active client projects do you typically manage at one time?" (1, 2, 3 to 5, 6+ — 3+ required)

*Pain points:*
3. "What is your biggest frustration with how you currently manage client projects?" (Open-ended)
4. "On a scale of 1 to 10, how organised do you feel your current project management system is?" (Rating)

*Concept evaluation:*
5. "Having read the description of Stackly, how likely are you to sign up for a free trial?" (5-point: Definitely would to Definitely would not)
6. "What appeals most about the concept?" (Open-ended)

*Pricing:*
7. "At what monthly price would Stackly be too expensive for you to consider?" (Numeric input)
8. "At what monthly price would Stackly seem like good value?" (Numeric input)

---

**SECTION 5: DATA COLLECTION PLAN (summary)**

Fieldwork runs 10 June to 20 June. Survey live via Typeform. Panel top-up via Prolific if we fall short of n = 150 by day five. Interviews scheduled as 45-minute video calls via Calendly, recorded with consent.

---

**SECTION 6: ANALYSIS APPROACH**

Quantitative: frequency distributions on all closed questions; cross-tabs by age band (25 to 30 vs 31 to 40) and by current tool used; Van Westendorp price sensitivity model on Q7 and Q8. Qualitative: thematic coding in Dovetail; three researchers each code independently then reconcile. We will also run the survey instrument through TestFeed first against a synthetic freelance designer audience to check question clarity and validate response distributions before going into field.

---

**SECTION 7: REPORTING FORMAT**

Slide deck (15 slides maximum) for the product team debrief on 28 June. One-page executive summary for the CEO. Full data tables in appendix. Internal use only.

## How to customise this template for your research

The template above covers the most common market research scenarios, but you will likely need to adjust a few things before it fits your project.

**Adjust the methodology section based on your timeline and budget.** If you have two weeks and no fieldwork budget, lean harder on secondary research and run your market research questions through a synthetic audience tool like [TestFeed](https://testfeed.ai) to get directional data fast. If you have eight weeks and a proper fieldwork budget, add in-depth interviews alongside your survey.

**Trim the question bank.** Section 4 above contains more questions than most single surveys should include. A good rule of thumb: every question you include should map directly to one of your stated research objectives. If it does not, cut it.

**Add a competitor analysis section if you are doing market sizing or strategic planning.** The basic template does not include a detailed competitor comparison grid. For a full [test audience](/blog/test-audience) or brand tracking study, you will want to add rows for two to four direct competitors and track awareness, consideration and preference for each.

**Use primary and secondary research together.** Secondary market research can tell you the size of the market and the broad trends. Primary market research tells you how your specific target audience thinks and behaves. The two methods answer different questions and are more powerful combined than either is alone. The [American Marketing Association](https://www.ama.org/topics/market-research/) has useful guidance on combining methods at different stages of a research programme.

**Pilot your survey before full launch.** Always test your survey instrument on five to ten people before sending it to your full sample. You are looking for questions that confuse people, answer options that do not fit, and surveys that run longer than ten minutes. This single step will meaningfully improve your data quality.

**Revisit your objectives last.** Once you have written all your questions, go back and check each one against your stated objectives. If a question does not contribute to an objective, remove it. If an objective has no questions addressing it, add them or remove the objective. This final check is what separates a well-designed study from a collection of interesting questions that produce no actionable output.

## Frequently asked questions

**What should a market research template include?**

At minimum: research objectives, a target audience definition, your chosen methodology (primary or secondary), survey or discussion questions, a data collection plan, an analysis approach, and a reporting format. A budget and timeline section is also worth adding for any study that involves external recruitment or fieldwork.

**What is the difference between primary and secondary market research?**

Primary market research means collecting new data directly from people, through surveys, interviews, focus groups or observation. Secondary market research means analysing data that already exists, such as industry reports, government statistics, competitor websites or academic studies. Most market research projects benefit from using both: secondary research to set the context, primary research to answer your specific questions.

**How many questions should a market research survey have?**

For most consumer surveys, ten to fifteen questions is a reasonable limit if you want completion rates above 70 per cent. Keep 70 to 80 per cent of questions closed-ended (multiple choice, rating scales) for easy analysis, and 20 to 30 per cent open-ended to capture qualitative context. The [SurveyMonkey research team](https://www.surveymonkey.com/mp/market-research-survey-templates/) consistently finds that surveys under ten minutes have significantly higher completion rates than longer ones.

**Can I use a synthetic audience instead of recruiting real respondents?**

Yes. Tools like TestFeed let you run your market research questions against a synthetic audience that reflects your target segment, giving you directional results in minutes rather than weeks. This works particularly well for early-stage idea validation, concept testing, and checking your survey instrument before you invest in full primary research fieldwork.
