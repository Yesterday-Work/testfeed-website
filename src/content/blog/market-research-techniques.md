The question determines the technique. Using a focus group when you need statistical confidence is as much a mistake as using a survey when you need to understand emotion and motivation.

There are ten market research techniques that cover the vast majority of business research questions. Each has a different profile of cost, speed, depth, and generalisability. Understanding that profile is what allows researchers to choose the right tool rather than defaulting to whatever is most familiar.

## The 10 market research techniques compared

| Technique | Type | Cost | Speed | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Surveys | Quantitative primary | Low–medium | Fast | Tracking, segmentation, concept testing at scale |
| In-depth interviews | Qualitative primary | Medium | Medium | Motivation, attitudes, exploratory work |
| Focus groups | Qualitative primary | Medium | Medium | Concept reactions, messaging, creative feedback |
| Observation | Qualitative primary | Medium–high | Slow | Behavioural context, product usage |
| Ethnographic research | Qualitative primary | High | Slow | Deep cultural or behavioural understanding |
| Secondary research | Secondary | Low | Fast | Market sizing, trends, competitive landscape |
| Competitive analysis | Secondary | Low | Fast | Positioning, product gap analysis, messaging gaps |
| Social listening | Secondary | Low–medium | Fast | Unprompted consumer sentiment, trend detection |
| A/B testing | Quantitative primary | Low | Fast | Optimising live assets — copy, design, price |
| Synthetic panel research | Quantitative primary | Low–medium | Very fast | Concept testing, price sensitivity, message testing |

## 1. Surveys

**Surveys** are the most widely used market research technique. They collect structured responses from a defined sample, allowing researchers to quantify attitudes, behaviours, and preferences at scale.

Surveys work best when the question is well defined, the response options are clear, and statistical generalisability matters. They are the workhorse of brand tracking, customer satisfaction measurement, segmentation studies, and concept screening.

Their main limitation is that they rely on self-reported data. What people say they do and what they actually do often diverge. Surveys also require a hypothesis: you must know what to ask, which makes them less useful for exploratory work.

## 2. In-depth interviews

**In-depth interviews** are one-on-one conversations, typically 45 to 90 minutes, structured around open-ended questions. They are the primary tool for understanding motivation, attitude formation, and the emotional context behind behaviour.

Interviews provide depth that surveys cannot. A skilled interviewer can probe unexpected responses, follow threads that reveal hidden needs, and surface the reasoning behind choices that a fixed-response survey would record but not explain.

The limitation is scale. A study of 20 in-depth interviews does not provide statistical confidence. The findings generate hypotheses and provide qualitative depth, but they need to be validated at scale through quantitative methods if decisions require statistical confidence.

## 3. Focus groups

**Focus groups** bring together 6 to 10 participants in a moderated discussion, typically lasting 90 minutes to two hours. They are particularly effective for concept testing, creative evaluation, and brand perception work, where the group dynamic produces reactions that individual interviews would not surface.

> "A focus group is not a survey with more people in the room. It is a different kind of data, designed to surface social dynamics and group-level reactions, not individual opinions at scale."

Focus groups are fast to run compared with ethnographic methods, but they are vulnerable to groupthink and dominant personalities. They work best as an input to hypothesis generation, not as standalone evidence for decisions.

## 4. Observation studies

**Observation research** involves watching how people behave in natural settings rather than asking them to report on their behaviour. In retail, this means watching how shoppers navigate a category. In digital research, it means studying session recordings or eye-tracking data.

Observation studies are particularly valuable when self-report data is unreliable, such as for habitual behaviour, fast decision-making at the point of purchase, or physical product interactions.

## 5. Ethnographic research

**Ethnography** involves spending extended time with a target audience in their natural environment, observing behaviour, routines, and cultural context. It is the most resource-intensive technique in market research, but it produces a depth of understanding that no other method can replicate.

Ethnographic research is most justified for product innovation work, where understanding the full context of a behaviour, not just a snapshot, is essential for designing something that fits into people's lives.

## 6. Secondary research

**Secondary research** analyses data that already exists: government statistics, industry reports, academic publications, competitor content, and internal business records. It is the fastest and cheapest way to understand market size, competitive landscape, and category trends.

Its limitation is specificity. Secondary data was collected for someone else's purpose. It may not map to your exact audience, geography, or time period. See our full guide on [secondary research](/blog/secondary-research/) for a detailed breakdown of sources and methodology.

## 7. Competitive analysis

**Competitive analysis** is a structured review of how competitors are positioning, pricing, messaging, and communicating in a category. It uses secondary sources — websites, product materials, advertising, press coverage, analyst reports — to build a picture of the competitive landscape.

Used alongside primary research, competitive analysis provides the context to interpret consumer feedback. A brand gap identified in a survey becomes more actionable when you can see exactly how a competitor is filling that gap.

## 8. Social listening

**Social listening** monitors unprompted consumer conversation across social media, review platforms, forums, and news sources. Unlike surveys, it captures what people say when they are not being asked. This makes it useful for detecting emerging sentiment, identifying unprompted category associations, and spotting problems before they show up in brand tracking data.

The limitation is that social media audiences are not representative samples. People who post publicly are different from people who do not, and the topics that generate social conversation are not necessarily the topics that drive purchase decisions.

## 9. A/B testing

**A/B testing** exposes different versions of an asset, a price, a headline, an email subject line, or a product page, to different user groups and measures which performs better. It is the most rigorous technique for optimising live assets because it tests actual behaviour rather than stated preference.

A/B testing requires sufficient traffic or sample volume to produce statistically significant results, and it can only test variations of something that already exists. It is not suited for exploratory work or for questions that require consumer explanation rather than behavioural measurement.

## 10. Synthetic panel research

**Synthetic panel research** uses AI-modelled audiences, calibrated to match the demographic and behavioural profile of a target population, to simulate consumer responses to research questions. Rather than recruiting real respondents through a panel, synthetic respondents are generated from models trained on real consumer data.

The practical advantage is speed. Concept testing, message validation, and price sensitivity research that would take weeks through a traditional panel can be completed the same day. Synthetic panels are most valuable for iterative research, where teams need to test multiple variants quickly, and for early-stage work where the cost of full primary research is not yet justified.

They are not a replacement for all primary research. For deeply qualitative questions, for studies that require genuine human experience, or for high-stakes decisions requiring statistical precision, real respondents remain the right choice. But for a growing range of quantitative research tasks, synthetic panels represent a meaningful shift in what is operationally possible.

## Choosing the right technique

The right choice is almost always determined by three questions: What type of data do you need (depth vs. scale)? How quickly do you need it? And what decision will it inform?

Most research programmes benefit from combining techniques. Use [exploratory research](/blog/exploratory-research/) to generate hypotheses, surveys to test them at scale, and secondary research to contextualise both. Adding synthetic panels compresses the timeline significantly without sacrificing the quality of the quantitative data.

## Frequently asked questions

## What are the main market research techniques?

The main techniques are surveys, in-depth interviews, focus groups, observation studies, ethnographic research, secondary research, competitive analysis, social listening, A/B testing, and synthetic panel research. The right technique depends on whether you need breadth or depth, speed or statistical rigour, and whether existing data can answer the question or new data is required.

## What is the most commonly used market research technique?

Surveys are the most commonly used technique because they combine reasonable cost, scale, and speed. They can collect both quantitative and qualitative data from large samples and are well-suited for tracking, segmentation, and concept testing. Their main limitation is that they rely on self-reported data, which can differ from actual behaviour.

## What is the difference between qualitative and quantitative market research techniques?

Qualitative techniques such as interviews and focus groups explore the reasons behind behaviour and produce rich, interpretive data. Quantitative techniques such as surveys and analytics measure the scale and frequency of behaviour and produce statistically generalisable data. Strong research programmes typically combine both.

## When should you use synthetic panels instead of traditional research techniques?

Synthetic panels are most useful when you need consumer insights quickly, when traditional panel recruitment would be expensive or slow, or when you want to test multiple concepts in rapid succession. They are well-suited for concept testing, price sensitivity research, message validation, and early-stage exploratory work.
