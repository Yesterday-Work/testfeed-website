CPG market research sits at the centre of every major decision a brand team makes. Whether you are evaluating a new flavour extension, testing a reformulated pack, or figuring out whether your launch price will hold, the quality of your research directly predicts the quality of the outcome. Yet for most mid-size CPG brands, the research process is still too slow, too expensive, and too disconnected from the speed at which the category moves.

This guide covers what CPG market research actually involves, which methods work best for common brand decisions, and how the tooling landscape has changed enough that small and mid-size FMCG teams can now run studies that were once exclusive to the largest manufacturers.

## What is CPG market research?

**CPG market research** is the systematic collection and analysis of consumer data to inform decisions about consumer packaged goods. It spans the full product lifecycle: from early-stage concept screening and product development, through pricing and packaging decisions, to post-launch brand tracking and competitive positioning.

Consumer packaged goods are, by definition, purchased frequently and replaced quickly. That velocity means that poor decisions compound fast. A pricing error, a claims statement that fails to convert, or a pack design that reads poorly on shelf can cost a brand months of distribution before the team even has confirmation that something is wrong.

Research is the mechanism for making those decisions before the investment is locked in.

The most common objectives for CPG consumer insights work include:

- **Concept testing:** Does this product idea have enough appeal to warrant investment?
- **Price sensitivity testing:** At what price does purchase intent fall below viability?
- **Packaging and claims testing:** Which design and which message drives the highest shelf conversion?
- **Segmentation:** Which consumer groups are most likely to buy, and what motivates them?
- **Brand tracking:** How is brand awareness, consideration, and preference shifting over time?

## Why CPG brands need dedicated research methods

Generic market research approaches do not translate cleanly to consumer packaged goods. Most off-the-shelf survey platforms are built for B2B software buyers or service experiences. CPG decisions involve category-specific complexity: purchase frequency, pack size trade-offs, shelf set dynamics, and the role of promotions in trial.

**FMCG insights** teams operate under constraints that make speed and repeatability non-negotiable. Category review cycles happen on fixed retailer timelines. Innovation pipelines run 12 to 18 months ahead of launch. A study that takes 10 weeks to field is often useless by the time it reports back.

There is also the cost problem. The established standard for CPG concept testing has long been Nielsen BASES, a volumetric forecasting system with four decades of normative data from over 300,000 product launches. BASES is accurate and well-understood. It is also priced at $50,000 to $150,000 per study with an 8 to 12 week timeline, according to multiple published comparisons. For a large CPG company testing 50 concepts per year, that is a material budget line. For a challenger brand or emerging FMCG player, it is simply not accessible.

The category has responded. A new generation of concept testing platforms and, more recently, synthetic panel research tools has made it possible to run CPG-grade research at a fraction of the traditional cost and timeline.

> "The question is no longer whether you can afford to do research. It is whether you can afford the speed penalty of doing it the old way."

## Key CPG market research methods

Understanding which method matches which business decision is the most important skill in CPG consumer research. The following methods cover the core toolkit for most brand and innovation teams.

### Concept testing

Concept testing evaluates consumer response to a product idea, usually represented as a written description, a rendered pack, or a brief stimulus. The goal is to assess appeal, relevance, purchase intent, and differentiation before committing to development costs.

For CPG brands, **monadic concept testing** is the gold standard. Each respondent sees a single concept and rates it independently, which avoids the contamination that comes from sequential or comparative designs where earlier concepts anchor perceptions.

A basic CPG concept test measures:

- Overall appeal (top-2 box score)
- Purchase intent (typically a five-point scale)
- Uniqueness and fit with brand
- Key driver analysis to identify which claims or attributes are doing the most work

### Price sensitivity testing

Two quantitative methods dominate pricing research in consumer packaged goods.

**Gabor-Granger** presents respondents with a product at a specific price and asks whether they would buy it. By testing across a range of price points with different respondent cells, you construct a revenue-maximising price curve. It is straightforward to run, fast to analyse, and well-suited to single-SKU decisions.

**Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter** asks four open-ended price questions: too cheap, cheap but acceptable, expensive but acceptable, and too expensive. The intersection of the acceptable range identifies a pricing corridor. It works well earlier in development when you are establishing range rather than optimising a specific point.

Both methods are natural fits for synthetic panel research, where you can rotate price stimuli across large respondent pools at low cost.

### Message and claims testing

CPG packaging carries a limited number of claims. Deciding which three or four messages deserve space on-pack is a high-stakes decision made, in most cases, without enough data.

Claims testing uses MaxDiff (maximum difference scaling) to identify which messages are most compelling to your target consumer. Respondents repeatedly choose the most and least appealing option from small sets of claims, producing a clear preference ranking that is statistically robust even with relatively small samples.

### Brand tracking

Brand tracking in CPG measures awareness, consideration, purchase, and brand health metrics at regular intervals. It is not a single study but a continuous programme, typically quarterly or monthly, that detects shifts in competitive position before they appear in scan data.

### Segmentation and audience profiling

Segmentation research identifies which consumer groups represent the highest-value targets. In CPG this usually means combining attitudinal and behavioural variables to find segments defined by occasion, need state, or category involvement rather than purely demographic factors.

## How to run CPG concept testing: a step-by-step guide

A well-structured CPG concept test follows a consistent process regardless of which platform you use.

**Step 1: Define the business decision**

The most common mistake in concept testing is writing the brief around "learning" rather than "deciding." Identify the decision you need to make before designing the study. Is this a go or no-go for development funding? A choice between two formulations? A decision about which positioning to take into shelf testing?

**Step 2: Define your target audience**

CPG concept tests should screen for category buyers, not general population respondents. A protein bar concept shown to people who never purchase in the health snacks category will produce artificially low purchase intent scores.

Define your audience by:
- Category purchase frequency (e.g., buys health snacks at least monthly)
- Pack size or format relevance
- Channel (grocery, convenience, DTC)

**Step 3: Build the concept stimulus**

For early-stage screening, a written concept board with product name, key benefit, and a single hero claim is sufficient. For later-stage validation, use a rendered pack visual or a finished prototype image.

Avoid overloading the stimulus. **One concept, one core claim, one reason to believe.** If you test everything at once you learn nothing about what is driving the response.

**Step 4: Design the questionnaire**

A standard CPG concept test questionnaire runs 10 to 15 minutes and covers:

- Screener questions (category purchase, brand familiarity)
- Concept exposure (stimulus shown)
- Appeal rating
- Purchase intent
- Uniqueness
- Key driver questions (open-ended or MaxDiff)
- Diagnostics (what would make you more likely to buy, what puts you off)

**Step 5: Choose your sample size and methodology**

For monadic concept tests, a minimum of 150 respondents per cell is the standard in CPG. If you are testing two concepts monadically you need at least 300 completes total.

For synthetic panel research, sample sizes can be larger at lower cost, which improves statistical confidence and allows for subgroup analysis by age, household type, or purchase occasion.

**Step 6: Field and analyse**

Set up quality controls: attention checks, speeder traps, open-ended response screening. CPG concept test data is only as good as its respondent quality.

Analyse top-2 box scores for appeal and purchase intent, and compare against category benchmarks where available. An appeal score of 60%+ top-2 box is typically considered strong in most CPG categories; purchase intent above 50% top-2 box warrants progression.

**Step 7: Report and decide**

Findings should map directly to the decision defined in Step 1. If the concept clears your threshold, proceed. If it does not, the diagnostic data from Steps 4 and 6 should tell you what to fix.

## Common mistakes CPG teams make in consumer research

Even well-resourced FMCG insights teams repeat the same research errors. These are the most consequential ones.

**Recruiting the wrong audience**

General population samples will suppress purchase intent for any niche or premium CPG product. Always screen for relevant category buyers. If your product is a functional beverage targeting active consumers aged 25 to 40, your research audience should reflect that, not the national adult population.

**Testing too many concepts at once**

Sequential concept exposure produces comparison effects. Respondents anchor on the first concept they see and evaluate everything else relative to it. Use monadic designs for clean, actionable data.

**Confusing claimed purchase intent with actual behaviour**

Purchase intent scores from surveys overstate real-world trial rates. The standard correction factor applied in CPG is to take 80% of "definitely would buy" and 30% of "probably would buy" responses. Do not present top-2 box intent scores as predicted trial rates without this adjustment.

**Running research too late to act on it**

The most common structural problem in CPG consumer insights is that research is commissioned after packaging artwork has been finalised or pricing has been set by finance. At that point the research can only confirm or create regret. Build research checkpoints into the innovation process early, not as a post-rationalisation step.

**Treating CPG research as a one-time event**

Consumer preferences, competitive context, and category norms shift continuously. A single concept test from 18 months ago is not a reliable foundation for a 2026 launch decision. Build regular research cadences, even lightweight synthetic panel checks, into your planning calendar.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is CPG market research?

CPG market research is the process of gathering and analysing consumer data to inform decisions about consumer packaged goods. It covers concept testing, pricing research, packaging and claims testing, brand tracking, and consumer segmentation, across the full product development and commercialisation lifecycle.

### How much does CPG market research cost?

Costs range from under $5,000 for a focused synthetic panel concept test to $150,000 or more for a full BASES-style volumetric forecast. Most agile quantitative platforms sit in the $5,000 to $25,000 range per study. Traditional agency qualitative work typically runs $15,000 to $50,000 depending on methodology and number of markets.

### What is the difference between CPG and FMCG market research?

The terms are functionally interchangeable. CPG is standard in North America; FMCG is used more widely in the UK, Europe, and Asia-Pacific. The research methods, objectives, and analytical frameworks are the same regardless of which term a team uses.

### What are the best concept testing platforms for CPG companies?

The strongest options for CPG-specific concept testing include Zappi (for automated normative benchmarking), Quantilope (for agile quantitative studies including conjoint), Toluna Start (for panel scale and speed), and TestFeed (for synthetic panel research at low cost and high speed). The right platform depends on whether you need normative data, volumetric forecasting, or fast directional signals.

### How does Gabor-Granger pricing work for CPG products?

Gabor-Granger presents each respondent with a product at a specific price and records whether they would purchase it. By testing across price increments with different respondent groups, you construct a demand curve showing how purchase intent changes with price. The revenue-maximising price point, where the product of price and predicted volume is highest, then informs the launch pricing decision.
